Our Detailed Termite Inspection Process

A meticulous inspection is the cornerstone of effective termite control. Our experienced technicians are trained to identify even the subtle signs of termite activity. During a typical inspection, we will examine both the interior and exterior of your property, focusing on areas that are particularly attractive to termites:

Exterior Inspection Areas

  • Foundation: We look for mud tubes, which termites use to travel between their colony and food source (your home).
  • Wood Structures: Decks, fences, and wood siding are carefully inspected for signs of damage or infestation.
  • Wood-to-Ground Contact: This is a common entry point for termites, and we examine areas where wood meets the soil.
  • Landscaping & Mulch: Certain landscaping materials can attract termites, and we assess their proximity to your home.

Interior Inspection Areas

  • Basements & Crawl Spaces: Dark, damp areas are often prime locations for termite activity.
  • Attics: Termites can also infest attics, particularly if there are leaks or moisture issues.
  • Wall Interiors: We listen for hollow sounds in walls, which can indicate termite damage.
  • Around Windows & Doors: Termites can enter through small cracks and crevices.

Following the inspection, we will provide you with a detailed report outlining our findings, the type of termites identified, and our recommended treatment plan. We take the time to explain everything clearly and answer any questions you may have.

Effective Termite Treatment Options

Based on our inspection findings, we will recommend the most appropriate and effective treatment method for your specific situation. Our goal is to eliminate the current infestation and provide long-term protection.

Liquid Termite Treatments

These treatments involve applying a liquid termiticide to the soil around the foundation of your home, creating a protective barrier that termites cannot penetrate. Liquid treatments can be highly effective for both eliminating active infestations and preventing future ones. The termiticide works by killing termites on contact and also through ingestion as they tunnel through the treated soil. Our technicians are trained to apply these treatments precisely, ensuring maximum effectiveness while minimizing impact on your property and the environment.

Termite Baiting Systems

Baiting systems utilize strategically placed bait stations around the perimeter of your property. Termites discover the bait, feed on it, and carry it back to their colony, sharing it with other termites. The active ingredient in the bait is a slow-acting termiticide that disrupts the termite molting process, ultimately leading to the demise of the entire colony. Baiting systems are an excellent long-term solution and can be used to monitor for future termite activity. This method is particularly effective for certain termite species and in environmentally sensitive areas.

Localized Treatments

In some cases, where termite activity is limited to a specific area, we may recommend localized treatments. These treatments target the infested area directly and may involve using foam or dust termiticides to eliminate termites within walls or other structures. Localized treatments are often used in conjunction with other methods to provide comprehensive protection.

Wood Treatments

Certain wood treatments can make wood less appealing to termites and protect it from damage. These treatments can be applied to existing wood structures or used as a preventative measure during construction. Borate-based treatments are a common and effective option for protecting wood from termites and other wood-destroying insects.

Common Problems, Signs, and Solutions for Termites in Post Falls

Termites are often called 'silent destroyers' because they can cause significant damage before their presence is even detected. Knowing the signs of a termite infestation is crucial for early intervention and minimizing the extent of damage. Here are some common problems associated with termites in Post Falls, the telltale signs to watch for, and how our expert services provide the solutions.

Finding Swarms of Termites

One of the most common signs of a termite infestation is the presence of swarming termites. Swarming is the reproductive stage of a termite colony, where winged termites, called 'swarmers,' emerge from the colony to establish new ones. Swarming typically occurs during warmer weather and often after rain. Seeing a swarm, or finding discarded wings near windowsills, door frames, or light fixtures, is a strong indicator of asubterranean termite infestation nearby, and it's time to call in the professionals for a thorough inspection. Our team can quickly identify the type of termite and locate the source of the swarm, allowing for targeted treatment to eliminate the active colony.

Discovering Mud Tubes

Subterranean termites require moisture to survive and build protective pathways, known as 'mud tubes,' to travel between their underground colony and their food source (your home's wooden structures). These tubes are often found on foundation walls, crawl spaces, or other vertical surfaces. They can range in size and texture but are typically made of soil, wood particles, and termite saliva. The presence of mud tubes is a definitive sign of a subterranean termite infestation, and they indicate that termites are actively accessing your property. Our inspection includes a careful search for mud tubes, and their discovery guides our treatment strategy to eliminate the colony and break the connection between the termites and your home.

Witnessing Damaged Wood

Termites feed on cellulose, the primary component of wood. As they consume wood from the inside out, the damaged wood may appear warped, sagging, or have a hollow sound when tapped. You might also notice small holes or galleries within the wood. Termite damage can occur in various wood structures, including support beams, flooring, walls, and even furniture. Ignoring damaged wood can lead to severe structural compromise over time, requiring costly repairs. Our team can assess the extent of the damage and recommend appropriate treatment to eliminate the termites and prevent further destruction. In addition to treatment, we can often recommend qualified contractors for any necessary repairs.

Noticing Termite Droppings (Frass)

'Frass' is the term for termite droppings, which are small, pellet-like excretions. Drywood termites, which live entirely within the wood they infest, often expel frass from small 'kick-out' holes in the wood. The appearance of frass can vary depending on the type of wood consumed but is often described as looking like sawdust or tiny dark pellets. Finding frass around wooden structures is a clear sign of a drywood termite infestation. Our specialists are trained to identify different types of termite frass and locate the infested wood, allowing for targeted treatment to eliminate the colony. Drywood termite infestations often require different treatment approaches than subterranean termites, highlighting the importance of accurate identification.

Experiencing Clicking Sounds in Walls

Sometimes, if you listen closely, you might hear faint clicking or rustling sounds coming from inside your walls. These sounds can be caused by termites actively feeding on the wood. While not always easy to detect, these sounds can be a subtle early warning sign of a termite infestation. If you suspect you hear such sounds, it's always best to have a professional inspection to confirm the cause and take appropriate action. Our experienced technicians have the tools and expertise to investigate such sounds and determine if termites are the culprit.

Identifying Soft or Sagging Floors/Ceilings

Significant termite damage to the structural wood supporting floors or ceilings can lead to noticeable sagging or a spongy feel when walked upon. This indicates a more advanced stage of infestation where termites have compromised the integrity of the wood. This type of damage requires immediate attention from a qualified termite control professional to prevent further deterioration and potential safety hazards. We can assess the extent of the structural damage and implement a treatment plan to eliminate the termites responsible. We can also provide guidance on necessary structural repairs.

Recognizing Blistering Paint or Wallpaper

Termites can cause moisture to build up behind paint or wallpaper as they tunnel through wood. This can lead to the appearance of blistering or peeling paint or wallpaper. While other factors can cause blistering, if it's accompanied by other signs of termite activity, it's a strong indicator of an infestation behind the surface. Our inspections include checking for these visual cues, particularly in areas prone to moisture or where wood is present.

Having Trouble with Sticky Windows or Doors

As termites consume wood, they can cause frames to warp, making windows and doors difficult to open or close. If you notice windows or doors that have suddenly become sticky or don't operate smoothly, and there are no other obvious reasons like humidity or settling, it could be a sign of termite damage to the frames. Termite infestations can happen in various parts of your home, and seemingly minor issues like sticky windows can be a clue to a larger problem. Our comprehensive inspections cover window and door frames as potential points of infestation.

Locating Piles of Termite Wings

As mentioned earlier, termite swarmers shed their wings after they swarm and attempt to establish a new colony. Piles of discarded wings, often found near windows, doors, or light sources, are a clear indication that a termite swarm has occurred. This means there is likely an active termite colony nearby that produced the swarm. Finding termite wings is a strong call to action to schedule a professional termite inspection immediately to prevent further infestation and damage. We can help you determine the origin of the swarm and implement effective control measures.

Understanding Termite Biology and Behavior

To effectively control termites, it's helpful to understand a bit about their biology and behavior. Termites are social insects that live in colonies with distinct roles: workers, soldiers, and reproductives. Workers are responsible for foraging for food (wood) and maintaining the colony. Soldiers protect the colony from threats. Reproductives, including the king and queen, are responsible for reproduction and expanding the colony. Understanding these roles and how termites interact within their colony helps our technicians develop targeted treatment strategies. For example, baiting systems exploit the workers' foraging behavior to distribute the bait throughout the colony.

Preventing Future Termite Infestations

While our primary focus is eliminating active infestations, we also provide advice and recommendations for preventing future termite problems. Preventive measures can significantly reduce your risk of infestation and save you from costly repairs down the line. Some common preventative tips include:

  • Reducing Moisture: Termites are attracted to moisture. Fix leaky pipes, faucets, and roofs. Ensure proper ventilation in crawl spaces and attics.
  • Removing attractants: Keep wood away from your foundation. Store firewood at least 20 feet away from your home and elevated off the ground.
  • Maintaining Your Landscape: Trim bushes and trees away from your home's foundation. Avoid using wood mulch directly against the foundation.
  • Sealing Entry Points: Seal any cracks or crevices in your foundation or walls that could provide access for termites.
  • Regular Inspections: Schedule regular professional termite inspections, even if you don't see any signs of infestation. Early detection is key to minimizing damage.

We are happy to discuss preventative strategies tailored to your property during our inspection or service call. Proactive measures are a cost-effective way to protect your valuable investment.
